Project Overview

A multi-phase collaborative project demonstrating versatility across digital and analog game formats. Built in Unreal Engine 5, the project progressed from a top-down shooter prototype to a refined first-person shooter, culminating in an analog board game adaptation that required translating real-time combat into strategic turn-based gameplay.

I designed modular weapon systems and dual-resource UI mechanics that adapted across both digital perspectives, then fundamentally reimagined these systems for tabletop play, converting AI behaviors into dice-based mechanics and real-time movement into tactical positioning.

My Contributions

  • Programmed modular weapon system with interchangeable components for flexible combat options
  • Designed and implemented dual-resource UI system providing real-time combat feedback
  • Integrated Niagara-based environmental hazards to increase level interactivity
  • Translated digital FPS mechanics into turn-based analog board game systems
  • Conducted peer code reviews and documented system architecture for team reference
  • Participated in daily standups and sprint planning using agile development practices

Challenges & Solutions

Challenge 1: Accessible Weapon Design

Team Feedback: "We need weapons that non-programmers can create easily."

The Solution: Created Blueprint parent-child architecture with exposed parameters (fire rate, damage, ammo), enabling designers to create weapon variants without programming knowledge. This accelerated content creation and proved essential when adapting systems between projects.

Challenge 2: Player Survivability Balance

Player Feedback: "I keep dying before I can learn anything."

The Solution: Used data-driven balancing: increased FPS health (5→30) and shields (10→20), reduced environmental damage (5→3), doubled top-down health (4→8), slowed enemy projectiles (100→60cm/s), and expanded pickups (3→8). Extended gameplay from 1-1.5 minutes to 5-10 minutes while maintaining challenge.

Challenge 3: Digital-to-Analog Translation

Team Feedback: "How do we convert real-time combat into turn-based gameplay?"

The Solution: Fundamentally reimagined core systems: AI enemies became dice-based player-controlled units with activation ranges, real-time movement converted to tactical dice rolls (Escaper: 2d6, Controller: 1d6), time-based buffs became turn-based durations, and 3D levels became modular board tiles. Implemented strict Controller limitations (max 6 traps, 6 enemies, 4 pickups) after playtesting revealed unlimited power created impossible scenarios, teaching valuable lessons about asymmetric balance and competitive constraints.

What I Learned

Data-driven iteration transformed my design approach. Making measurable adjustments—like increasing kamikaze speed from 150cm/s to 500cm/s based on playtesting—proved more effective than subjective tuning. This analytical methodology now informs all my balancing decisions.

Implementing 18+ communication enhancements taught me that effective design requires layered feedback across visual, auditory, and tactile channels. Players navigated complex systems independently when feedback reinforced understanding through multiple senses simultaneously.

The analog adaptation revealed that core design principles transcend medium. While digital excelled through immediate feedback and spatial navigation, analog required strategic planning and constraint-based design. Converting every system—AI to dice mechanics, movement to tactical positioning, time-based to turn-based—fundamentally changed how I evaluate what makes mechanics engaging in their specific medium.

Creating modular parent-child systems proved essential for cross-project adaptation. Flexible architecture meant weapon systems, enemy behaviors, and UI elements transferred between perspectives rather than requiring rebuilds, shifting my approach from implementation-first to experience-first thinking.

FPS Development Insights

The team implemented 15+ unique mechanics including a dual health/shield system, three distinct enemy types (flying, ranged, and kamikaze), environmental hazards, weapon pickups, power-ups, breakable walls, shifting doors with pressure plates, and a checkpoint system. Through rigorous dev playtesting, we balanced difficulty by adjusting enemy placement, increasing player survivability (health: 5→30, shields: 10→20), fine-tuning movement physics for platforming sections, and reducing environmental damage.

  • → All team members unanimously agreed the FPS surpassed our previous top-down shooter
  • → Better level design and more polished mechanics enhanced player immersion
  • → Efficient use of reusable assets allowed deeper focus on gameplay refinement
  • → 18 communication enhancements transformed functional gameplay into cohesive player experience

Top-Down Shooter Development Insights

Our team's first collaborative project served as the foundation for our later FPS work, featuring 11 unique gameplay elements including mouse-driven aiming, two distinct enemy types, stationary turrets with adjustable AI parameters, environmental spike hazards, health and speed boost pickups, and a 30-wave survival system.

Key Development Insights

  • → Early-stage prototyping focused on establishing foundational combat systems
  • → Iterative balancing through quantitative playtesting data (speed values, health counts, wave duration)
  • → Shift from defensive to aggressive gameplay through strategic pickup placement
  • → Foundation established for modular systems later refined in FPS project
