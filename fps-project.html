Project Overview

A collaborative multi-phase project built in Unreal Engine 5, showcasing our team's versatility across different gameplay perspectives and mediums. This comprehensive project included three distinct deliverables: a first-person shooter, a top-down shooter adaptation, and an analog board game conversion called "Call of Board." Each phase demonstrated agile development practices, modular system design, and our ability to translate core mechanics across different formats.

Working in an agile team environment with Anthony Tran, Kira Stout, Seth Chambers, and Vincent Campbell, I focused on creating flexible weapon systems and dual-resource UI mechanics that could adapt across both digital shooter perspectives. The first-person shooter emphasized responsive combat mechanics and environmental navigation, while the top-down variation challenged us to recontextualize the same systems from an overhead perspective, requiring adjustments to camera systems, player feedback, and spatial awareness.

The analog "Call of Board" adaptation represented our most ambitious design challenge—translating real-time FPS mechanics into turn-based tabletop gameplay. This conversion introduced asymmetric player roles (Escaper vs. Controller), dice-based combat resolution, and strategic board layout design. The exercise deepened our understanding of fundamental game design principles that transcend medium, revealing how pacing, player agency, and strategic depth fundamentally shift between real-time digital and turn-based analog formats.

Game Mechanics & Development Log

This document details the core gameplay systems and iterative design process for the FPS project. The team implemented 15+ unique mechanics including a dual health/shield system, three distinct enemy types (flying, ranged, and kamikaze), environmental hazards, weapon pickups, power-ups (invincibility), breakable walls, shifting doors with pressure plates, and a checkpoint system. Through rigorous dev playtesting, the team balanced difficulty by adjusting enemy placement, increasing player survivability (health: 5→30, shields: 10→20), fine-tuning movement physics for platforming sections, and reducing environmental damage. All five team members unanimously agreed the FPS game surpassed their previous top-down shooter, citing better level design, more polished mechanics, enhanced player immersion, and efficient use of reusable assets that allowed deeper focus on gameplay refinement.

Mechanics & Iteration Log

This document chronicles the development of our top-down shooter prototype, featuring 11 unique gameplay elements including mouse-driven aiming and WASD movement, two distinct enemy types (normal ranged and kamikaze), stationary turrets with adjustable AI parameters, environmental spike hazards, health and speed boost pickups, and a 30-wave survival system. Through extensive dev playtesting, the team iteratively balanced the experience by slowing enemy projectiles (100→60cm/s), dramatically increasing kamikaze threat level (150→500cm/s movement speed), tripling player survivability (4→8 max health), expanding health pickups (3→8), adding tactical turret placement (1→5 turrets with refined 115° line-of-sight), and strategically distributing speed boosts (1→3 pickups with 5-second duration). These data-driven adjustments transformed the initial prototype from a static, defensive experience into a dynamic, movement-focused arena shooter that encouraged aggressive player positioning and strategic resource management.

Call of Board - Analog Game Adaptation

As a creative design exercise, our five-person team (Anthony Tran, Joshua Silva, Kira Stout, Seth Chambers, and Vincent Campbell) translated our digital first-person shooter into a strategic tabletop board game. This adaptation challenged us to convert real-time FPS mechanics into turn-based tactical gameplay while maintaining the core experience of navigating enemy-filled environments.

Call of Board introduces a unique 2-player asymmetric experience where one player becomes the "Escaper" (navigating the board) while the other acts as the "Controller" (designing the board layout and controlling enemies). This competitive dynamic replaced the single-player digital experience with strategic player-versus-player confrontation, adding extensive replayability through variable board layouts.

Digital-to-Analog Translation Challenges

Converting our first-person shooter required fundamental rethinking of core systems. The biggest challenge wasn't simply adapting existing mechanics—it was creating entirely new rules and objectives that captured the essence of the digital experience in an analog format.

  • → Enemy AI to Player Control: Simple AI-controlled enemies became Controller-managed units with activation ranges (4-6 tiles) and dice-based damage systems (Normal: 6HP/0-3 damage, Flying: 4HP/0-2 damage, Kamikaze: 3HP/1-2 damage)
  • → Real-Time to Turn-Based: Fast movement inputs converted to dice roll movement (Escaper: 2d6, Controller: 1d6) with tactical positioning replacing twitch reflexes
  • → Time-Based to Turn-Based Buffs: Invincibility changed from 5-second duration to 3-turn protection, requiring new balance considerations
  • → Level Design to Board Layout: Multiple digital rooms became modular map tiles (1-4 tiles per game) with Controller-imposed limitations (max 6 traps, 6 enemies, 4 pickups per tile)
  • → Vertical Movement Loss: 3D navigation reduced to 2D grid movement, fundamentally changing strategy and pacing

Key Design Insights from Playtesting

Initial playtesting revealed that unlimited Controller power created impossible scenarios. We implemented strict limitations on obstacle placement to balance competitive fairness while maintaining strategic depth. This taught us valuable lessons about asymmetric game balance and the importance of constraints in competitive design.

Materials Used: The game utilized everyday objects (bottle caps, coins, pen caps, salt/pepper packets) as game pieces, demonstrating resourceful analog prototyping. This DIY approach made the game accessible while maintaining clear visual differentiation between component types.

Conclusion: While we concluded the digital version was ultimately more engaging due to its fast-paced action and vertical movement—core features lost in translation—the analog adaptation deepened our understanding of fundamental game design principles that transcend medium. The exercise revealed how game feel, pacing, and strategic depth fundamentally change when shifting from real-time to turn-based systems.

Challenges & Solutions

Challenge: Designing a weapon system that was both modular and easy for team members to extend.

Solution: Created a Blueprint-based parent weapon class with configurable parameters (fire rate, damage, ammo capacity) that allowed designers to create weapon variants without programming knowledge. This accelerated content creation and empowered the entire team.

Challenge: Coordinating development across multiple team members working on interconnected systems.

Solution: Established clear communication protocols through daily standups, used Perforce for version control, and created comprehensive documentation for all shared systems. This minimized merge conflicts and kept everyone aligned.

What I Learned

This project reinforced the importance of clear communication and documentation in team environments. Working with programmers and artists taught me how to design systems that are both technically sound and accessible to non-technical team members.

I also gained valuable experience in agile methodologies, learning how to break large features into manageable tasks, prioritize work effectively, and adapt to changing requirements. The collaborative nature of this project strengthened my ability to give and receive constructive feedback.
