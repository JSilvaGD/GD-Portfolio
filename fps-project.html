Project Overview

A collaborative multi-phase project built in Unreal Engine 5, showcasing our team's versatility across different gameplay perspectives and mediums. This comprehensive project included three distinct deliverables: a first-person shooter, a top-down shooter adaptation, and an analog board game conversion called "Call of Board." Each phase demonstrated agile development practices, modular system design, and our ability to translate core mechanics across different formats.

Working in an agile team environment with Anthony Tran, Kira Stout, Seth Chambers, and Vincent Campbell, I focused on creating flexible weapon systems and dual-resource UI mechanics that could adapt across both digital shooter perspectives. The first-person shooter emphasized responsive combat mechanics and environmental navigation, while the top-down variation challenged us to recontextualize the same systems from an overhead perspective, requiring adjustments to camera systems, player feedback, and spatial awareness.

The analog "Call of Board" adaptation represented our most ambitious design challenge‚Äîtranslating real-time FPS mechanics into turn-based tabletop gameplay. This conversion introduced asymmetric player roles (Escaper vs. Controller), dice-based combat resolution, and strategic board layout design. The exercise deepened our understanding of fundamental game design principles that transcend medium, revealing how pacing, player agency, and strategic depth fundamentally shift between real-time digital and turn-based analog formats.

üé≤ Call of Board - Analog Game Adaptation

As a creative design exercise, our five-person team (Anthony Tran, Joshua Silva, Kira Stout, Seth Chambers, and Vincent Campbell) translated our digital first-person shooter into a strategic tabletop board game. This adaptation challenged us to convert real-time FPS mechanics into turn-based tactical gameplay while maintaining the core experience of navigating enemy-filled environments.

Call of Board introduces a unique 2-player asymmetric experience where one player becomes the "Escaper" (navigating the board) while the other acts as the "Controller" (designing the board layout and controlling enemies). This competitive dynamic replaced the single-player digital experience with strategic player-versus-player confrontation, adding extensive replayability through variable board layouts.

Digital-to-Analog Translation Challenges

Converting our first-person shooter required fundamental rethinking of core systems. The biggest challenge wasn't simply adapting existing mechanics‚Äîit was creating entirely new rules and objectives that captured the essence of the digital experience in an analog format.

  • ‚Üí Enemy AI to Player Control: Simple AI-controlled enemies became Controller-managed units with activation ranges (4-6 tiles) and dice-based damage systems (Normal: 6HP/0-3 damage, Flying: 4HP/0-2 damage, Kamikaze: 3HP/1-2 damage)
  • ‚Üí Real-Time to Turn-Based: Fast movement inputs converted to dice roll movement (Escaper: 2d6, Controller: 1d6) with tactical positioning replacing twitch reflexes
  • ‚Üí Time-Based to Turn-Based Buffs: Invincibility changed from 5-second duration to 3-turn protection, requiring new balance considerations
  • ‚Üí Level Design to Board Layout: Multiple digital rooms became modular map tiles (1-4 tiles per game) with Controller-imposed limitations (max 6 traps, 6 enemies, 4 pickups per tile)
  • ‚Üí Vertical Movement Loss: 3D navigation reduced to 2D grid movement, fundamentally changing strategy and pacing

Key Design Insights from Playtesting

Initial playtesting revealed that unlimited Controller power created impossible scenarios. We implemented strict limitations on obstacle placement to balance competitive fairness while maintaining strategic depth. This taught us valuable lessons about asymmetric game balance and the importance of constraints in competitive design.

Materials Used: The game utilized everyday objects (bottle caps, coins, pen caps, salt/pepper packets) as game pieces, demonstrating resourceful analog prototyping. This DIY approach made the game accessible while maintaining clear visual differentiation between component types.

Conclusion: While we concluded the digital version was ultimately more engaging due to its fast-paced action and vertical movement‚Äîcore features lost in translation‚Äîthe analog adaptation deepened our understanding of fundamental game design principles that transcend medium. The exercise revealed how game feel, pacing, and strategic depth fundamentally change when shifting from real-time to turn-based systems.

Challenges & Solutions

Challenge 1: Modular Weapon System Design

The Problem: Designing a weapon system that was modular and accessible to team members across both FPS and top-down perspectives without requiring extensive programming knowledge.

The Solution: Created a Blueprint-based parent weapon class with configurable parameters including fire rate, damage, and ammo capacity. This parent-child architecture allowed designers to create weapon variants by adjusting exposed variables while maintaining consistent interaction feedback. The modular approach proved essential when adapting systems between projects, demonstrating the value of reusable architecture. Team members could create new weapons efficiently, significantly accelerating content creation.

Challenge 2: Player Survivability Balance

The Problem: Initial playtests revealed players were eliminated within 1-2 minutes, creating frustration that prevented them from learning enemy patterns or exploring level design.

The Solution: Implemented comprehensive balancing adjustments based on playtesting feedback. In the FPS project, we increased base health from 5 to 30 and shields from 10 to 20, while reducing environmental damage from 5 to 3 per tick. In the top-down shooter, we doubled max health from 4 to 8, reduced enemy projectile speed from 100cm/s to 60cm/s, and increased health pickup placement from 3 to 8 distributed across the map. These adjustments extended average gameplay time from 1-1.5 minutes to 5-10 minutes while maintaining challenge, demonstrating the value of data-driven design decisions.

Challenge 3: Player Communication Systems

The Problem: Players struggled to understand pickup functions, environmental interactions, and game state. Playtesters reported uncertainty about item purposes and required constant verbal guidance to understand mechanics.

The Solution: Implemented 18+ communication enhancements combining visual, auditory, and screen feedback. Visual improvements included color-coded UI elements matching pickups, bullet icons replacing text, directional indicators on interactive elements, and unique textures for breakable objects. Audio cues included weapon sounds, collection feedback for all pickups, and environmental audio for moving objects. Screen effects provided immediate feedback for damage, healing, and power-up activation. Post-implementation playtesting revealed significant improvements, with players navigating complex systems independently and reporting the enhancements made dangerous elements immediately clear.

Challenge 4: Enemy Threat Balancing

The Problem: Enemy types failed to create meaningful tactical decisions. Kamikaze enemies at 150cm/s were easily avoided, while certain sections overwhelmed players. Players either ignored specific enemy types or were eliminated with limited counterplay options.

The Solution: Differentiated enemy types through distinct behaviors and adjusted parameters based on playtesting. Kamikaze enemy speed increased from 150cm/s to 500cm/s, making them immediate priority threats. The FPS project featured three distinct enemy types with unique attack patterns, with density balanced by reducing one section by 4 enemies and increasing another by 12. Stationary turrets increased from 1 to 5 with refined 115¬∞ line-of-sight, creating varied tactical challenges that required adaptive player strategies.

Challenge 5: Checkpoint System Implementation

The Problem: Players experienced frustration from restarting lengthy levels after mistakes. Playtesting identified the absence of checkpoints as a critical issue causing players to abandon rather than retry.

The Solution: Implemented a checkpoint system using trigger boxes that save player position and update spawn locations dynamically. Checkpoints were strategically placed between major level sections, transforming the experience from punishing to challenging but fair. This allowed players to experiment with approaches, learn enemy patterns, and take calculated risks. The implementation reinforced the importance of respecting player time investment in maintaining engagement.

Challenge 6: Team Coordination & Version Control

The Problem: Coordinating development across five team members working on interconnected systems while maintaining version control proved challenging. Early development encountered conflicts when multiple members modified shared assets.

The Solution: Established clear communication protocols through daily coordination, used Perforce for version control, and created comprehensive documentation for all shared systems. Each team member maintained primary responsibility for specific mechanics with clear ownership: weapons and UI systems, enemy AI and screens, power-ups and checkpoints, environmental mechanics, and turrets and audio. Documentation included detailed implementation notes and balance justifications. This structure minimized conflicts and ensured team understanding of system interfaces.

Challenge 7: Encouraging Dynamic Player Movement

The Problem: Players adopted passive defensive patterns, maintaining maximum distance in the top-down shooter and camping positions in the FPS. Levels felt static with players avoiding optional areas and rewards.

The Solution: Redesigned pickup distribution and power-up duration to incentivize aggressive positioning. Health pickups increased from 3 to 8 non-respawning items, encouraging map exploration. Speed boost pickups adjusted from one 12-second duration to three 5-second bursts, placed strategically to create risk-reward decisions. Movement physics were tuned‚Äîjump velocity adjusted from 450cm/s to 475cm/s, air control boost from 25 to 100, and falling friction from 0 to 2‚Äîenabling optional platforming challenges. These changes transformed both games into dynamic experiences where positioning and exploration provided tangible advantages.

What I Learned

Data-Driven Iterative Design

This project transformed my approach to game balance. Rather than relying on subjective impressions, our team made specific, measurable adjustments based on playtesting data‚Äîenemy projectile speeds, movement velocities, health values, pickup counts, and damage rates‚Äîwith documented reasoning for each change. For example, discovering that kamikaze enemies at 150cm/s were easily avoided led to a precise increase to 500cm/s, making them immediate priority threats. Concrete metrics enabled clear team communication and created reproducible results. When balancing issues arose, the solution became systematic playtesting with measurable outcomes and validation testing. This analytical approach, where decisions are justified with data and reasoning, will inform all future projects.

Player Communication Through Integrated Feedback

Working on 18+ communication enhancements demonstrated that effective game design communicates through multiple sensory channels simultaneously. Before implementing comprehensive feedback systems, players struggled with pickup identification and environmental interactions. After adding color-coded UI elements, collection sounds, screen effects, and environmental indicators, playtesting revealed players navigated complex systems independently. The most significant insight came from observing how layered feedback‚Äîvisual confirmation, audio spatial awareness, and screen state communication‚Äîworked together to create understanding without explicit instruction. This holistic approach to player communication, where interactions are reinforced through sight, sound, and feel, will be foundational in future design work. Players should learn through play, not tutorials.

Designing for Reusability and Adaptation

The progression from top-down shooter to FPS project demonstrated the value of modular, reusable systems designed with future adaptation in mind. Creating flexible parent classes with adjustable variables meant weapon systems, enemy behaviors, and UI elements could be adapted between projects rather than rebuilt. This approach proved efficient and essential for iterative development. The analog adaptation further reinforced this lesson, showing how understanding core mechanics enables translation across entirely different mediums. The fundamental question became: what is the essential experience we're creating, and how can different systems deliver that experience? This shift from implementation-first to experience-first thinking has permanently influenced how I approach design challenges.

The Importance of Diverse Playtesting

Documentation revealed a critical insight: playtesting must include both experienced gamers and novices to reveal the full spectrum of player experience. Experienced players breezed through content and identified skill-based exploits, while less experienced players struggled with controls and specific challenges. These contrasting experiences demonstrated that a single difficulty curve cannot satisfy everyone, but effective communication systems help bridge the skill gap by making core mechanics understandable regardless of gaming literacy. I learned to design for accessibility first‚Äîensuring novice players understand core systems through clear feedback‚Äîthen add optional challenges for experienced players. This human-centered approach‚Äîtesting with real people representing different target audience segments‚Äîis now essential to my development process.

Collaborative Development and Clear Ownership

Working in a five-person team across interconnected systems demonstrated that clear ownership prevents chaos while enabling collaboration when combined with comprehensive documentation and communication protocols. Each team member maintained primary responsibility for specific mechanics with explicit attribution. This structure established decision ownership and consultation points when work intersected. Comprehensive documentation, including specific numerical changes and reasoning, enabled team members to understand decisions made asynchronously and build upon them confidently. This disciplined approach‚Äîcombining clear ownership with thorough documentation, regular coordination, version control discipline, and systematic feedback integration‚Äîwill be essential for future collaborative projects.

Technical Growth Through Hands-On Implementation

This project provided invaluable technical growth through practical implementation. Creating custom particle systems, learning animation blueprints and blend spaces, implementing adjustable AI parameters, and developing dual-resource UI systems all required diving into documentation, testing edge cases, and iterating on feel. Game development tools reward experimentation‚Äîthe most effective learning comes from building with real production constraints, deadlines, and team dependencies. The team's unanimous agreement that this project surpassed previous work suggests our collective technical skills improved significantly through collaborative development. This reinforced that technical mastery comes from repeated practice with real production constraints rather than isolated exercises.
