Project Overview

A collaborative multi-phase project built in Unreal Engine 5, showcasing versatility across different gameplay perspectives and mediums. This comprehensive project included three distinct deliverables: a first-person shooter, a top-down shooter adaptation, and an analog board game conversion, all demonstrating agile development practices and modular system design.

Working in an agile team environment, I focused on creating flexible weapon systems and dual-resource UI mechanics that could adapt across both digital shooter perspectives. The FPS emphasized responsive combat and environmental navigation, while the top-down variation required adjustments to camera systems, player feedback, and spatial awareness. The final phase challenged us to translate our digital FPS into a turn-based analog board game, requiring fundamental rethinking of real-time mechanics into strategic tabletop gameplay.

  • Programmed modular weapon system with interchangeable components for flexible combat options

Challenges & Solutions

Challenge 1: Modular Weapon System

Team Feedback: "We need weapons that non-programmers can create and modify easily."

The Solution: Created a Blueprint-based parent weapon class with configurable parameters including fire rate, damage, and ammo capacity. This parent-child architecture allowed designers to create weapon variants by adjusting exposed variables while maintaining consistent interaction feedback. Team members could create new weapons efficiently, significantly accelerating content creation and proving essential when adapting systems between projects.

Challenge 2: Player Survivability

Player Feedback: "I keep dying in the first minute before I can learn anything" and "The game feels too punishing."

The Solution: Implemented comprehensive balancing adjustments based on playtesting data. In the FPS, I increased base health from 5 to 30 and shields from 10 to 20, while reducing environmental damage from 5 to 3 per tick. In the top-down shooter, I doubled max health from 4 to 8, reduced enemy projectile speed from 100cm/s to 60cm/s, and increased health pickups from 3 to 8. These adjustments extended average gameplay time from 1-1.5 minutes to 5-10 minutes while maintaining challenge.

Challenge 3: Player Communication

Player Feedback: "I don't know what these pickups do" and "How do I know if something is dangerous?"

The Solution: Implemented 18+ communication enhancements combining visual, auditory, and screen feedback. Visual improvements included color-coded UI matching pickups, bullet icons replacing text, directional indicators on interactive elements, and unique textures for breakable objects. Audio cues included weapon sounds, collection feedback, and environmental audio. Screen effects provided immediate damage and healing feedback. Post-implementation playtesting revealed players navigated complex systems independently without verbal guidance.

Challenge 4: Digital-to-Analog Translation

Team Feedback: "How do we convert real-time combat into turn-based gameplay?" and "The analog version feels impossible to balance."

The Solution: Fundamentally rethought core systems rather than directly translating mechanics. AI-controlled enemies became Controller-managed units with activation ranges (4-6 tiles) and dice-based damage systems. Fast movement inputs converted to dice roll movement (Escaper: 2d6, Controller: 1d6), replacing twitch reflexes with tactical positioning. Time-based buffs like invincibility changed from 5-second duration to 3-turn protection. Multiple digital rooms became modular map tiles (1-4 tiles per game) with strict Controller limitations (max 6 traps, 6 enemies, 4 pickups per tile) to prevent impossible scenarios. Initial playtesting revealed unlimited Controller power created unfair situations, teaching us valuable lessons about asymmetric game balance and the importance of constraints in competitive design.

Challenge 5: Team Coordination

Team Feedback: "We're running into version control conflicts" and "I'm not sure who's working on what."

The Solution: Established clear communication protocols through daily coordination, used Perforce for version control, and created comprehensive documentation for all shared systems. Each team member maintained primary responsibility for specific mechanics with clear ownership. Documentation included detailed implementation notes and balance justifications. This structure minimized conflicts and ensured team understanding of system interfaces.

What I Learned

This project transformed my approach to game balance through data-driven iterative design. Rather than relying on subjective impressions, our team made specific, measurable adjustments based on playtesting—enemy speeds, health values, pickup counts—with documented reasoning. For example, discovering kamikaze enemies at 150cm/s were easily avoided led to increasing them to 500cm/s, making them immediate threats. This analytical approach will inform all future projects.

Working on 18+ communication enhancements demonstrated that effective design communicates through multiple sensory channels simultaneously. The most significant insight came from observing how layered feedback—visual confirmation, audio spatial awareness, and screen state communication—worked together to create understanding without explicit instruction. Players should learn through play, not tutorials.

The progression from top-down shooter to FPS demonstrated the value of modular, reusable systems. Creating flexible parent classes with adjustable variables meant weapon systems, enemy behaviors, and UI elements could be adapted between projects rather than rebuilt. This shift from implementation-first to experience-first thinking has permanently influenced how I approach design challenges.

The analog adaptation revealed how fundamental game design principles transcend medium. While we concluded the digital version was ultimately more engaging due to fast-paced action and vertical movement—core features lost in translation—the exercise deepened my understanding of pacing, strategic depth, and player agency. Converting real-time combat into turn-based gameplay required rethinking every system: AI became player-controlled units with dice mechanics, movement became tactical positioning, and time-based buffs became turn-based durations. The biggest lesson was understanding what makes a game engaging in its medium—the digital version succeeded through immediate feedback and spatial navigation, while the analog version required strategic planning and constraint-based design. This cross-medium perspective has fundamentally changed how I evaluate core game mechanics.

Working in a five-person team demonstrated that clear ownership prevents chaos when combined with comprehensive documentation and communication protocols. Each team member maintained primary responsibility for specific mechanics. This disciplined approach—combining clear ownership with thorough documentation, regular coordination, and version control—will be essential for future collaborative projects.

FPS Development Insights

The team implemented 15+ unique mechanics including a dual health/shield system, three distinct enemy types (flying, ranged, and kamikaze), environmental hazards, weapon pickups, power-ups, breakable walls, shifting doors with pressure plates, and a checkpoint system. Through rigorous dev playtesting, we balanced difficulty by adjusting enemy placement, increasing player survivability (health: 5→30, shields: 10→20), fine-tuning movement physics for platforming sections, and reducing environmental damage.

  • → All team members unanimously agreed the FPS surpassed our previous top-down shooter
  • → Better level design and more polished mechanics enhanced player immersion
  • → Efficient use of reusable assets allowed deeper focus on gameplay refinement
  • → 18 communication enhancements transformed functional gameplay into cohesive player experience

Top-Down Shooter Development Insights

Our team's first collaborative project served as the foundation for our later FPS work, featuring 11 unique gameplay elements including mouse-driven aiming, two distinct enemy types, stationary turrets with adjustable AI parameters, environmental spike hazards, health and speed boost pickups, and a 30-wave survival system.

Key Development Insights

  • → Early-stage prototyping focused on establishing foundational combat systems
  • → Iterative balancing through quantitative playtesting data (speed values, health counts, wave duration)
  • → Shift from defensive to aggressive gameplay through strategic pickup placement
  • → Foundation established for modular systems later refined in FPS project
