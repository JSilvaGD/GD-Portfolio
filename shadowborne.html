Project Overview

Shadowborne is my comprehensive team project made within a span of 4 months, demonstrating advanced gameplay design and QA methodologies. The project focuses on creating immersive combat experiences with responsive systems and environmental storytelling, bridging design vision with technical implementation.

As both Gameplay Designer/Programmer and QA Lead, I designed level layouts, implemented gameplay systems using Blueprint visual scripting, and ensured quality through rigorous testing documentation. The project emphasizes player engagement through precision-timed combat mechanics and adaptive AI systems.

My Contributions

  • Optimized Blueprint performance across multiple gameplay systems to maintain consistent frame rates
  • Created adaptive enemy AI systems that scale difficulty based on player performance
  • Implemented responsive UI/UX healing systems using Blueprint for seamless player feedback
  • Developed precision-timed combat mechanics that reward player skill and timing
  • Designed immersive level layouts emphasizing environmental storytelling, pacing, and player engagement
  • Led QA documentation through Jira, tracking bugs and coordinating fixes across development cycles
  • Conducted iterative playtesting sessions and gathered actionable feedback

Challenges & Solutions

Challenge 1: Blueprint Performance

Player Feedback: "Frame drops make timing dodges impossible."

The Solution: Used Unreal's profiling tools to identify bottlenecks, then converted Blueprint tick functions to event-driven architecture using event dispatchers. Optimized collision detection with appropriate channels and simplified shapes. Batched UI updates to once per frame and implemented object pooling for projectiles and damage numbers. These Blueprint-focused optimizations increased frame rate from 45fps to stable 60fps during peak combat.

Challenge 2: UI/UX Feedback Systems

Player Feedback: "I didn't realize I was taking damage until I died."

The Solution: Implemented layered feedback using UMG widgets and Blueprint: directional damage indicators showing attack direction and color-coded severity, screen vignette effects intensifying with damage, dynamic health bar animations with color transitions, healing particle effects with audio cues, and floating damage numbers. Post-implementation playtesting showed players accurately assessed health status without explicitly checking UI.

Challenge 3: Combat Difficulty Balance

Player Feedback: "Early enemies feel too easy, but later encounters are overwhelming."

The Solution: Implemented enemy behavior variations using Blueprint that adjusted attack cooldowns, movement speed, and health based on player performance in previous encounters. Tracked combat duration and damage taken through Blueprint timers, then adjusted subsequent spawns accordingly. Added visual tells through enemy stances and attack telegraph timing that subtly communicated difficulty changes. Iterative playtesting refined these parameters until the curve felt consistently challenging but fair across different skill levels.

Challenge 4: Solo QA Leadership

Team Feedback: "We keep finding the same bugs weeks apart."

The Solution: Established Jira workflow with four priority tiers (Critical, High, Medium, Low) and standardized bug report templates requiring reproduction steps, expected behavior, and screenshots. Implemented verification checklists requiring retesting of related systems to catch regression bugs. Scheduled dedicated two-hour testing blocks three times weekly separate from development time. This reduced recurring bugs by 75% and kept the team focused on completion-critical issues.

What I Learned

Adaptive Systems Require Subtle Design: The most successful aspect of difficulty balancing was its transparency. Players attributed changes to their own skill rather than algorithmic adjustments, maintaining agency and satisfaction. This taught me that the best game systems are invisible—players unconsciously appreciate them without consciously noticing the support mechanisms.

QA as Design Feedback Loop: Leading QA while programming revealed that testing isn't just bug-catching—it's critical design validation. Structured testing exposed design assumptions that didn't survive player interaction. Designing with testing in mind from the start made systems inherently easier to debug and verify, making me a more thorough, systems-aware designer.

Performance Directly Impacts Feel: Before optimization, players blamed themselves for missed timing. After achieving stable 60fps, those same players succeeded at identical mechanics simply because frame drops no longer interfered. This demonstrated that "game feel" isn't purely animation and input response—it's fundamentally about eliminating technical friction between player intent and on-screen result.

Feedback Systems Enable Learning: Players can only master mechanics they accurately perceive. When health changes happened silently, players couldn't learn from mistakes. After implementing layered feedback—directional indicators, vignette intensity, damage numbers—players naturally developed pattern recognition and tactical awareness. Good feedback design transforms confusion into learning opportunities through clear cause-and-effect communication.

Dual Roles Build Holistic Understanding: Managing both programming and QA simultaneously created comprehensive pipeline understanding that single-discipline focus couldn't provide. As a programmer, I wrote more testable code. As QA lead, I appreciated implementation complexity and wrote actionable bug reports. This dual perspective made me a better cross-discipline communicator, able to speak credibly to both designers and programmers.
